The Residue Theorem

Complex analysis studies functions f:U→ℂf\colon U \to \mathbb{C} that are differentiable in the complex sense, known as holomorphic functions. Such functions are extraordinarily rigid: holomorphicity on an open set implies infinite differentiability and a convergent power series representation. Isolated points where this regularity breaks down are called singularities, and their local behaviour is captured precisely by the Laurent series expansion.

Definition 3.1. Let ff be holomorphic on a punctured disk 0<|z−a|<r0 < |z - a| < r. The residue of ff at aa is the coefficient a−1a_{-1} in the Laurent expansion f(z)=∑n=−∞∞an(z−a)n, f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n (z - a)^n, equivalently, Res⁡(f,a)=12πi∮|z−a|=εf(z)dz. \operatorname{Res}(f, a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{|z-a|=\varepsilon} f(z)\,dz. For a simple pole, the residue simplifies to Res⁡(f,a)=limz→a(z−a)f(z). \operatorname{Res}(f, a) = \lim_{z \to a}(z - a)f(z).

Theorem 3.2 (Residue Theorem). Let U⊂ℂU \subset \mathbb{C} be open and ff holomorphic on UU except at finitely many isolated singularities a1,…,an∈Ua_1, \ldots, a_n \in U. For any positively oriented simple closed contour γ\gamma in UU avoiding all aka_k, ∮γf(z)dz=2πi∑k=1nn(γ,ak)Res⁡(f,ak), \oint_{\gamma} f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i \sum_{k=1}^{n} n(\gamma, a_k)\,\operatorname{Res}(f, a_k), where n(γ,ak)n(\gamma, a_k) denotes the winding number of γ\gamma about aka_k.

The integrand has simple poles at z=±1z = \pm 1, both enclosed by |z|=2|z| = 2. Computing each residue: Res⁡(1z2−1,1)=limz→1z−1z2−1=limz→11z+1=−12,Res⁡(1z2−1,−1)=limz→−1z+1z2−1=limz→−11z−1=−12. \begin{aligned} \operatorname{Res}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{z^{2}-1},\; 1\right) &= \lim_{z \to 1} \frac{z - 1}{z^{2}-1} = \lim_{z \to 1} \frac{1}{z+1} = \phantom{-}\frac{1}{2}, \\ \operatorname{Res}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{z^{2}-1},\; {-1}\right) &= \lim_{z \to -1} \frac{z + 1}{z^{2}-1} = \lim_{z \to -1} \frac{1}{z-1} = -\frac{1}{2}. \end{aligned} The residues sum to zero, so I=2πi⋅0=0I = 2\pi i \cdot 0 = 0.

The residue theorem is one of the most powerful computational tools in complex analysis, reducing contour integrals over closed curves to finite algebraic sums. Its applications extend well beyond pure mathematics: it underlies partial fraction methods in signal processing, closed-form evaluation of real improper integrals, and aspects of the spectral theory of linear operators. The next section turns to a related but broader integration theorem, formulated in the language of differential forms on smooth manifolds.

Stokes’ Theorem

Stokes’ theorem is the central result of the calculus of differential forms, unifying the classical theorems of Green, Gauss, and Stokes under a single statement. Its formulation requires the language of smooth manifolds and the exterior derivative dd, which generalises the gradient, curl, and divergence of vector calculus to arbitrary dimension and codimension.

Theorem 4.1 (Stokes’ Theorem — General Form). Let Ω\Omega be a smooth oriented kk-manifold with boundary ∂Ω\partial\Omega, and let ω\omega be a smooth (k−1)(k-1)-form on Ω\Omega. Then ∫∂Ωω=∫Ωdω, \int_{\partial\Omega} \omega = \int_{\Omega} d\omega, where dd denotes the exterior derivative.

In classical vector calculus, taking Ω\Omega to be a surface S⊂ℝ3S \subset \mathbb{R}^{3} with boundary curve ∂S\partial S and ω=𝑭⋅d𝒓\omega = \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} recovers the familiar vector form: ∮∂S𝑭⋅d𝒓=∬S(∇×𝑭)⋅d𝑺. \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_{S} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}.

Corollary 4.2 (Green’s Theorem). For a positively oriented simple closed curve ∂D\partial D bounding a region D⊂ℝ2D \subset \mathbb{R}^{2} and smooth functions PP, QQ, ∮∂D(Pdx+Qdy)=∬D(∂Q∂x−∂P∂y)dxdy. \oint_{\partial D} \bigl(P\,dx + Q\,dy\bigr) = \iint_{D} \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dx\,dy.
